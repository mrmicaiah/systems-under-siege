The garage. The place where things go to wait — wait to be used, wait to be fixed, wait to be dealt with "someday."

Our garage hasn't fit a car in two years. I'm not exaggerating. Twenty-four months of parking in the driveway, scraping ice off windshields in winter, getting soaked during rainstorms, all because the garage had become a storage unit for everything our family couldn't make a decision about.

Lucas's tools and half-finished project supplies. Joey's outgrown bike and the scooter with one broken wheel. Gracie's baby gear we kept "in case" even though we're definitely done having kids. Holiday decorations in seventeen different bins, none of them labeled. At least four boxes I couldn't identify without opening them.

The garage was where decision-avoidance went to multiply.

---

## BEFORE: THE FULL TRUTH

I want to be honest about how bad it was, because if your garage looks like ours did, you need to know you're not alone.

There was a path. One narrow walking path from the door to the shelves on the back wall, winding around boxes and bins and inexplicable piles. The rest was a solid mass of stuff, stacked and shoved and balanced in ways that defied physics. Things had fallen at some point and been left where they landed because moving them would require moving twelve other things first.

I found, during the eventual purge: a broken vacuum we'd replaced three years ago, two printers (neither worked), a bin of VHS tapes (we don't own a VCR), Lucas's college textbooks from a degree he finished fifteen years ago, a treadmill we used exactly twice, and a box labeled "MISC KITCHEN" that contained nothing related to a kitchen.

We'd been telling ourselves we'd deal with it "this weekend" for approximately one hundred weekends in a row. There was always something more pressing, something easier, something that didn't require confronting two years of accumulated avoidance.

Then one Thursday evening in October, it started raining while I was pulling into the driveway. Not a drizzle — a downpour. I sat in the car watching water sheet down the windshield, knowing I was about to get completely soaked walking to the house, staring at the garage that was fifteen feet away and completely useless.

I was done. The garage was getting purged that weekend, no matter what.

---

## THE STRATEGY

I'd tried to clean out the garage before. Twice, actually. Both times I'd gotten overwhelmed within the first hour, made a small dent, and given up. The problem was that I'd approached it like regular cleaning — picking things up, putting them somewhere else, making decisions one agonizing item at a time.

The Great Purge is different. It's one of my Reclaiming Territory methods, and it's built for exactly this kind of overwhelming, years-in-the-making disaster. The key is having a system that removes decision fatigue and forces immediate action.

Here's how it works:

**The Four-Box Method**

Before you touch anything, set up four clearly labeled zones. I used cardboard boxes for smaller items and designated floor areas for larger things:

1. **KEEP** — Stays in the garage, will get a proper home
2. **TOSS** — Broken, damaged, garbage, no longer functional
3. **DONATE** — Works fine, we just don't need it
4. **RELOCATE** — Belongs somewhere else in the house

Every single item goes into one of these four categories. No "maybe" pile. No "decide later" zone. Every item, one decision, into a box.

**The Rules**

I set ground rules before we started:

- If it's broken and hasn't been fixed in six months, it goes to Toss. We're not fixing it.
- If we haven't used it in two years, it goes to Donate unless it's seasonal (holiday decorations get a pass).
- If we don't remember what it is or why we have it, it goes to Donate or Toss.
- Decisions happen in under thirty seconds. If we're debating longer than that, the answer is probably Donate.
- Nothing comes back out of a box once it's in there.

**The Timeline**

I blocked the entire Saturday. Not "a few hours" — the whole day. Lucas took the morning shift with me while the kids watched movies inside. Jean agreed to take Joey and Gracie for the afternoon so we could finish without interruption. I scheduled a donation pickup for Sunday morning so the Donate pile couldn't linger and tempt us to reconsider.

## THE PROCESS

We started at 8 AM with coffee, trash bags, and a bluetooth speaker playing music loud enough to drown out any emotional attachment to broken printers.

**Phase 1: The Obvious Trash (2 hours)**

First pass was just garbage — things that were clearly broken, damaged, or actual trash that had somehow accumulated. This was psychologically important because it created visible progress fast. Two hours in, we'd filled eight trash bags and could see floor space we hadn't seen in months.

**Phase 2: The Keep/Donate Sort (3 hours)**

This was the hard part. We went shelf by shelf, pile by pile, touching every item exactly once and making a decision. Lucas struggled with his project supplies and tools. "I might need this someday" came out of his mouth approximately forty times. I kept pointing at the rules we'd written on a whiteboard: If you haven't used it in two years, it goes to Donate.

I struggled with the kids' outgrown stuff. Gracie's first bike. Joey's baby toys. Things that held memories even though they held no practical value. I let myself keep three truly sentimental items and made myself donate the rest. Someone else's kid could use Gracie's first bike. Keeping it in our garage wasn't honoring the memory — it was just hoarding.

**Phase 3: The Relocate Run (1 hour)**

Everything in the Relocate box got walked into the house and put in its actual home. Holiday decorations that had been living in unmarked boxes went into labeled bins and onto designated shelves. Tools that belonged in Lucas's workbench area went there. Items that belonged in the house but had drifted to the garage went back inside.

**Phase 4: The Removal (1 hour)**

This is the step that makes The Great Purge actually work. Before we stopped for the day, everything in Toss went to the curb for trash pickup, and everything in Donate went into the car to be dropped off first thing Sunday morning.

If you leave the Donate pile in the garage "for now," you will start pulling things back out. You will second-guess decisions you made when you were being ruthless and clear-headed. The stuff has to leave your property the same day, or The Great Purge becomes The Great Reshuffling.

## AFTER & MAINTENANCE

Our car fits in the garage now. I cannot overstate how much this has improved my quality of life. No more scraping ice, no more running through rain, no more watching the car bake in summer sun.

But a purged space only stays purged if you change the behavior that created the problem. Here's what we do now:

**The One-In-One-Out Rule:** If something new comes into the garage, something old has to leave. Lucas bought new drill bits? Old ones get donated or tossed.

**Monthly Walk-Through:** On the first Saturday of each month, I spend fifteen minutes scanning the garage for creeping piles. If something's been sitting there for a month without a purpose, it gets dealt with immediately.

**No "For Now" Items:** Nothing goes in the garage "for now" or "until I figure out where it belongs." If it doesn't have a designated spot on the shelves, it doesn't live in the garage.

The garage will try to become a dumping ground again. That's just its nature. But The Great Purge isn't a one-time event — it's a skill. And now that I've done it once, I know I can do it again if I need to.

Just hopefully not for another ten years.
