Marie called at 2:47 PM on a Tuesday.

"I'm in the area! Thought I'd stop by and bring you some things I found at the estate sale. You're going to love them."

Twenty minutes. I had twenty minutes.

I did a mental inventory of the house. Living room: acceptable. Kitchen: dishes in dishwasher, counter wiped this morning. Guest bathroom...

The guest bathroom.

I couldn't remember the last time I'd actually cleaned it. Joey uses it. Gracie uses it. Neither of them has ever voluntarily wiped a surface in their lives.

I had twenty minutes, a bathroom that looked like a science experiment, and a mother-in-law who would absolutely use that bathroom.

---

## THE QUICK FIX

Here's what I've learned about panic cleaning: without a plan, you'll spend twenty minutes fluttering around accomplishing nothing. You'll wipe one thing, notice another thing, start that, get distracted, and when the doorbell rings, you'll have touched seventeen surfaces and finished zero.

The 15-Minute Strike is the opposite of fluttering.

It's one zone. One timer. Focused attack. Done.

I grabbed my phone, set the timer for 15 minutes, and headed to the bathroom with my cleaning caddy. Here's exactly what I did:

**Minutes 1-3: The Toilet**

Marie will use this toilet. This is non-negotiable.

I sprayed the bowl with toilet cleaner, let it sit while I wiped down the seat, lid, and exterior with a disinfecting wipe. Quick swish with the brush. Flush. Done.

Not deep-cleaned. Presentable.

**Minutes 4-6: The Sink and Counter**

I swept everything on the counter into the cabinet underneath. Toothpaste globs, Joey's collection of hair ties he uses for who-knows-what, a rubber duck that's been there since August. All of it — shoved in the cabinet.

Sprayed the counter and sink. Wiped. The faucet had water spots, so I hit it with a dry microfiber cloth for ten seconds. Shiny enough.

**Minutes 7-9: The Mirror**

Here's the thing about mirrors: they're the first thing people notice. A spotless mirror makes the whole bathroom feel cleaner.

Glass cleaner. Paper towel. Thirty seconds of actual wiping, not just smearing the spots around.

**Minutes 10-12: The Floor (Visible Parts Only)**

I did not mop. I did not get on my hands and knees.

I grabbed a wet wipe and hit the floor around the toilet and in front of the sink. The parts Marie would actually see while standing there. The corner behind the door? Ignored. The area behind the toilet? Ignored.

Visible surfaces only.

**Minutes 13-15: The Finishing Touches**

Fresh hand towel. Straightened the shower curtain. Checked that there was toilet paper on the roll (there wasn't — grabbed a new roll from under the sink). Tossed the trash that was overflowing with... I didn't look too closely. Just tied the bag and replaced it.

Timer went off at 14:52.

I stopped.

This is the part that's hardest for me. The timer goes off and I want to just do one more thing. Fix that one spot I noticed. Organize under the sink since I'm already here.

No.

The 15-Minute Strike only works if you actually stop. Otherwise it becomes a 45-minute guilt spiral where you're still cleaning when the doorbell rings and you're sweaty and resentful.

I stopped. I walked away. The bathroom wasn't perfect. But it was presentable.

---

## THE RESULTS

Marie arrived, used the bathroom, and said nothing. Which is the goal. No comments about the state of the bathroom means the bathroom passed.

Here's what the 15-Minute Strike bought me:

**What I accomplished:**
- Clean toilet (the essential)
- Clear counter and clean sink
- Spotless mirror
- Visible floor wiped
- Fresh towel and full toilet paper roll
- Empty trash

**What I didn't do:**
- Scrub the grout
- Clean behind the toilet
- Organize under the sink
- Wash the bath mat
- Deep clean anything

And that's fine. That's the whole point.

The 15-Minute Strike isn't about getting the bathroom guest-ready for a home tour. It's about triage. It's about visible surfaces and functional fixtures. It's about creating the impression of a maintained bathroom without losing your entire afternoon to cleaning anxiety.

Fifteen minutes. One zone. Timer enforced.

Marie brought me a ceramic rooster and a set of doilies from the estate sale. I now own a ceramic rooster. But at least she didn't see the bathroom I'd been ignoring for three weeks.

## YOUR TURN

Next time you have fifteen minutes before someone arrives — anyone, not just your mother-in-law — try this:

1. Pick ONE zone (bathroom, kitchen, living room)
2. Set an actual timer for 15 minutes
3. Focus on visible surfaces only
4. Stop when the timer rings

Don't do one more thing. Don't spiral into deep cleaning. Just stop, walk away, and let "good enough" be good enough.

The 15-Minute Strike isn't about having a clean house. It's about having a clean-enough zone in the time you actually have.

Marie's coming. The timer's ticking. You've got this.
