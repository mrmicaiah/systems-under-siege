## Why This Method Exists

I used to think my closet problem was that I didn't have enough clothes. Every morning I'd stand in front of a closet stuffed so full the hangers couldn't move, and I'd think, "I have nothing to wear."

So I'd buy more clothes. A new top because the old ones weren't working. Another pair of jeans because surely the right jeans would fix everything. A dress for "someday" when I'd need something nice. The closet got fuller. The problem got worse.

It took me an embarrassingly long time to realize the issue wasn't quantity. The issue was that I was keeping things that didn't actually work for my life — clothes that didn't fit, didn't flatter, didn't suit my actual daily existence as a work-from-home mom who spends most of her time in yoga pants and oversized cardigans.

The Joy Test exists because I needed a way to decide what stays and what goes. Not based on some abstract concept of whether something "sparks joy," but based on whether it actually earns its space in my closet and my life.

---

## How I Discovered This

I'd read the organizing books. I knew the famous question: "Does it spark joy?" And I tried it, I really did. I held up a shirt and asked myself if it sparked joy, and the answer was... I don't know? It's a gray shirt. It's fine. Joy seems like a strong word for a gray shirt.

The problem with "spark joy" is that it works great for some things and terribly for others. My favorite cozy sweater? Joy, absolutely. The five nearly identical black tank tops I rotate through constantly? They don't spark joy, but they're essential to my daily life. My tax documents? Zero joy, but I'd be in serious trouble without them.

I needed a different framework. Something that accounted for the practical reality of what I actually need versus what I'm keeping out of guilt, wishful thinking, or pure inertia.

One Saturday morning, after another frustrating "I have nothing to wear" moment, I pulled everything out of my closet and dumped it on the bed. Lucas walked past, saw the mountain of fabric, and wisely said nothing except, "I'll take the kids to the park."

Three hours later, I'd developed what I now call The Joy Test — though it probably should be called The Practical Reality Test, which is less catchy but more accurate.

## The Method Step-by-Step

The Joy Test is a modified decision framework for what earns space in your home. It's not about minimalism or perfection. It's about intentionality — keeping what serves your actual life and releasing what doesn't.

The core question isn't "Does this spark joy?" It's: **"Does this earn its space?"**

That question breaks down into three sub-questions, asked in order:

**Question 1: Does this serve my current life?**

Not my past life. Not my imagined future life. My actual, right-now, today life.

The silk blouse I wore to client meetings when I worked in an office? That was my past life. I work from home now. No one sees me except Lucas, the kids, and occasionally the Amazon delivery driver. The silk blouse doesn't serve my current life.

The "goal jeans" I've been keeping for when I lose fifteen pounds? That's my imagined future life. I've had those jeans for four years. If future-me ever needs jeans in that size, future-me can buy jeans that are actually in style. The goal jeans don't serve my current life.

Be honest here. Brutally honest. Does this item get used? Does it get worn? Does it serve a real function in your actual daily existence?

**Question 2: Does this earn its space?**

Space is finite. Every item in your closet takes up room that could be empty, breathable, accessible. An item earns its space by being useful enough, loved enough, or necessary enough to justify the real estate it occupies.

My wedding dress is in my closet. I will never wear it again. It serves no practical function. But it earns its space because it's irreplaceable, meaningful, and I'm not ready to let it go. That's a valid reason to keep something.

The three bridesmaid dresses from friends' weddings? They don't earn their space. I wasn't emotionally attached, I'd never wear them again, and they were taking up prime closet real estate out of pure guilt. Gone.

**Question 3: If I got rid of this and needed it later, could I replace it?**

This question handles the "but what if" anxiety. The winter coat you wear every year? Hard to replace quickly if you got rid of it and needed it. Keep it. The random cardigan you haven't worn in two years but might theoretically want someday? Easy to replace, cheap to replace, and you've survived two years without reaching for it. Let it go.

Most "what if" items fall into the easily replaceable category. We keep them because getting rid of things feels risky, not because we actually need them.

**The Decision Flow:**

For each item, ask:
1. Does this serve my current life? If no → it goes.
2. Does this earn its space? If no → it goes.
3. If I'm unsure: Could I replace this if I needed it? If yes → it probably goes.

Move quickly. Don't agonize. Your first instinct is usually right. If you're debating for more than thirty seconds, the answer is probably "no, it doesn't earn its space" and you're just trying to talk yourself into keeping it.

## Real-Life Application

That Saturday with the clothes mountain on my bed, I went through every single item using the three questions. Here's what I found:

**The easy nos:** Clothes that didn't fit, were stained or damaged, or hadn't been worn in over two years. These were about 40% of my closet, and deciding on them took almost no time. The two-year rule is helpful here — if you haven't worn it in two full cycles of seasons, you're not going to.

**The guilt items:** Clothes I kept because they were expensive, gifts from family members, or purchases I regretted but felt bad getting rid of. These were the hardest. I kept reminding myself that the money was already spent whether I kept the item or not, and that keeping a gift I never wore didn't honor the giver any more than donating it to someone who would actually use it.

**The sentimental items:** My wedding dress, a t-shirt from my college years, Lucas's old flannel that I stole and never gave back. These earned their space through emotional value, not practical function. I kept them, but I was honest about why.

**The "someday" items:** Clothes for the body I used to have, the job I might get, the lifestyle I imagined. These were the sneakiest category because they felt like hope. But hope doesn't earn closet space. I let them go.

By the end, I had donated four garbage bags of clothes and kept maybe 60% of what I'd started with. My closet had breathing room. I could see everything I owned. And the next morning, I stood in front of it and actually knew what to wear, because everything in there was something I would actually wear.

## Your Turn

You don't have to do your whole closet in one day. Start with one section — just the hanging shirts, or just the jeans, or just the shelf of sweaters.

Pull everything from that section out. Put it on the bed where you can see it all. Then go through each item with the three questions:

1. Does this serve my current life?
2. Does this earn its space?
3. If I got rid of it and needed it later, could I replace it?

Move quickly. Trust your instincts. Put the keeps back, bag the donations immediately, and get the bags out of your house the same day if possible — otherwise you'll start second-guessing and pulling things back out.

The Joy Test isn't about having less stuff. It's about having the right stuff. The stuff that actually works for the life you're actually living, not the life you used to have or the life you imagine you'll have someday.

Your closet should serve you. If it's not, it's time to ask some hard questions.
