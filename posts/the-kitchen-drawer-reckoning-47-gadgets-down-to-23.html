The Kitchen Drawer Reckoning (47 Gadgets Down to 23)

February 3, 2026 · Category Conquest

The Battle Zone

The kitchen drawers. Specifically, the gadget drawer, the utensil drawer, and what I've been calling "the overflow drawer" for three years like that's a normal thing to have.

These drawers haven't been properly organized since we moved in. I've shoved things in, shoved things around, and occasionally rage-slammed them when they wouldn't close. The junk has achieved sentience. It fights back.

Every time I cook, I spend the first five minutes digging through a tangle of whisks, spatulas, and mystery tools I don't remember buying. Last week I cut my finger on something sharp buried under the potato masher. I still don't know what it was.

This is the kitchen equivalent of that closet everyone has. Except I open these drawers multiple times a day and mutter "why do we HAVE this" while searching for a simple measuring spoon.

Before: The Full Truth

Saturday afternoon. Taco night. I needed to open a can of black beans.

Simple task. Should take four seconds.

I opened the gadget drawer. Dug past the garlic press. Moved the apple corer. Shoved aside something that might be for removing strawberry stems? I've never removed a strawberry stem in my life.

No can opener.

I tried the utensil drawer. Spatulas. Wooden spoons. A meat thermometer I forgot we owned. Something called a "citrus reamer" that Lucas bought during his brief cocktail phase in 2019.

No can opener.

I checked the overflow drawer. And there, buried under a fondue fork set still in the packaging, I found not one can opener, but four.

Four can openers. In the same drawer. That I somehow never saw while looking for a can opener.

I lined them up on the counter and tried each one.

The first one: handle broke off in 2021, apparently. Still in the drawer.

The second one: manual, but the gear is stripped. Spins without gripping.

The third one: electric, except the battery compartment is corroded.

The fourth one: actually works, but the blade is so dull it took me two full minutes to open one can of beans.

Joey wandered in during this process. "Why do we have four can openers?"

"Excellent question, Joseph. I'll be spending my Saturday finding out."

Gracie appeared behind him. "Are those ALL can openers? That's so many can openers."

"Thank you for that observation."

"You should throw some away."

"I'm aware."

The Strategy

Here's what I've learned from decluttering other areas: cleaning drawer by drawer doesn't work. You organize one drawer, feel accomplished, and never realize you have the same stuff in three other locations.

This is where Category Conquest comes in.

Instead of organizing "the gadget drawer," you gather ALL kitchen gadgets from EVERYWHERE and put them in one place. Every drawer. The back of every cabinet. That basket on top of the fridge. The random items that migrated to the dining room buffet.

All of it. One pile. Then you see what you actually own.

It's horrifying. It's also the only way to make real decisions.

My game plan:

Step 1: Empty every kitchen drawer onto the counter and island.

Step 2: Pull gadgets from cabinets, the pantry shelf, anywhere else they're hiding.

Step 3: Group everything by type—all can openers together, all spatulas together, all "what even IS this" items together.

Step 4: For each category, keep only what I actually use. Not "might use." Not "could use if I suddenly became a person who makes fondue." Actually use.

Step 5: Find homes for the keepers. Donate or trash the rest.

Time allocated: One Saturday afternoon while Lucas took the kids to the park. I gave myself three hours. It took four, but I'm getting ahead of myself.

The goal wasn't a Pinterest kitchen. The goal was opening a drawer and finding what I need in under ten seconds.

Low bar. And yet.

The Process

I started by emptying the gadget drawer onto the island.

Then the utensil drawer.

Then the overflow drawer.

Then I remembered the cabinet next to the stove where I shoved the specialty items.

Then I remembered the basket on top of the fridge.

By the time I finished gathering, my entire kitchen island was covered. I could not see the countertop. Gracie would have said it looked like a gadget yard sale, and she would have been right.

The grouping revealed some uncomfortable truths.

Can openers: 4 (keeping 0, buying 1 that actually works)

Garlic presses: 3 (one from our wedding, one I bought because I forgot we had one, one Lucas bought because he didn't know where the other two were)

Spatulas: 11 (ELEVEN. For a family of four.)

Whisks: 4 (I use one. The tiny one. The other three have been "backup whisks" for eight years.)

Specialty single-use gadgets I've never used: A mango cutter. An avocado slicer. A strawberry huller. An herb stripper. A corn kernel remover. A pineapple corer still in the box.

Mystery items: 6. I had to Google two of them. One was an apple corer/slicer combination. One was a jar opener grip. The other four remain unidentified and were sacrificed to the donation box.

The hardest part wasn't deciding what to toss. It was confronting how this happened in the first place.

Every single item made sense when I bought it. "This garlic press has a BETTER GRIP." "This whisk is silicone so it won't scratch pans." "I'll DEFINITELY make fresh mango salsa."

I have never made fresh mango salsa. Not once.

Meanwhile, the tools I actually use every day—my one good spatula, my one good wooden spoon, my battered measuring cups—were buried under aspirational purchases.

Joey texted from the park: "Dad wants to know if you're done yet."

I was not done.

I was standing in front of a pile of 11 spatulas, trying to identify which four I actually use and feeling personally attacked by the evidence of my own optimism.

After and Maintenance

The final count:

Before: 47 kitchen gadgets/utensils across 3 drawers and 2 cabinets
After: 23 items, all in 2 drawers

I donated a full bag. Trashed the broken stuff. Kept only what I actually reach for when cooking.

It's been three weeks. I have opened the utensil drawer approximately 400 times, and every single time I've found what I needed in under five seconds.

Is it perfect? No. Joey already shoved a random plastic spork in there from his lunch box. Gracie contributed a tiny whisk from her play kitchen because "it matches, Mama."

But the system holds because there's room for these small invasions. When the drawers aren't stuffed to breaking, one rogue spork doesn't collapse everything.

The real test will be six months from now. My plan: every time I'm tempted to buy a "better" version of something I already own, I make myself throw away the current one first.

No more backup garlic presses.

No more "just in case" can openers.

One tool per job. And if that tool breaks, THEN I replace it.

Lucas's only comment when he saw the finished drawers: "Oh nice. Now I can find the bottle opener."

We have one bottle opener now.

Just one.

It's a miracle.
